Key Design Choices

Using Familiar Design Components

Ease of use by building off established design components was a main factor I incorporated in order to attract a typically younger user base. For this reason I chose full width buttons with bright colours and card views to reduce the learning curve involved during first use.

User Testing

Method

Users were sent a link to the prototype and an explanation giving them the background of the apps purpose (shared task list for multi-person homes). A questionnaire was provided to them to be filled out after they finished exploring the prototype to identify gaps between user expectations and designed features.

Results

Insights

Users need explicit hints for non-trivial functionality

The reoccurring chore option, edit home button and other users button were not explained but had what we thought were intuitive labels attached to them. These proved ineffective, where explicitly labelled buttons such as the “create a task” button worked perfectly fine.

Colour holds more significance than icons

Users were able to find their personal settings page but had difficulty finding the “Edit Home” and “Other Person’s Task List” page despite their close proximity. The key attribute of their personal settings button was the colour, which drew attention away from the rest of the carousel.

Final Product

Key Visual Design Choices

We chose to use shadows for important components over borders and lines to draw attention to the fact that these components are their own entities, and as such hold their own significance.

We chose a red accented theme to emphasize that the interactions taking place in this app hold more significance than other places on their phone, and also to remind users that those they are interacting with are loving individuals with whom they share a home.
